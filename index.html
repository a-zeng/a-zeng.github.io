MMX CAD Team

An online collaborative effort of 140+ volunteers to optimize the loading of a 6,000+ part assembly, pushing the limits of Fusion 360 and requiring a massive PDM database to manage.

The Marble Machine X (MMX) has been crazy musician Martin Molin's dream for the past 4 years, and I've been following along his journey on YouTube since the release of his first Marble Machine back in 2016. These kinetic machines use dropped marbles to strike various instruments and play music. When he announced in June 2020 that he was looking for CAD help from the community, I instantly jumped in and I haven't regretted it one bit.

A render of the Marble Paths Pre-assembly (the assembly I am team leader of)

The MMX is modeled in Fusion 360, originally incorporating every bolt, screw, and nut that would be used in its mechanical construction. This, however, is resource-intensive and taxing for Fusion 360 to run, so the CAD team's main goal has been to defeature the assembly by removing unnecessary parts and simplifying modeled features. This process is intended to lose data on the parts, since the main priority of this first phase is to reduce loading times for the master assembly.

Drum Marble Track

A Pan Around the Drum Marble Track

Objectives:
  • Defeature and simplify the Drum Marble Track
  • Update it to reflect the angles of the current design on the machine
Challenges:
  • Working around Fusion's poor implementation of 3D sketches and lines
  • Detection of lines for 3D sweeps
  • Aligning parts with other parts
Lessons Learned:
  • 3D lines need to be on the same sketch in order for Fusion to detect them as a tangent chain of lines
  • Avoid using 3D sweeps whenever possible
  • Use a master sketch to govern part alignment

Demagnetizing (Demag) Wheel

A Pan Around the Demagnetization Wheel

Objectives:
  • Defeature and simplify the Demag Wheel
  • Remove extra magnets in the center
Challenges:
  • Combining features of all the plates into as few sketches as possible to reduce loading complexity
Lessons Learned:
  • With captured design history, the more features that are present, the larger the file will be and the more time it will take to load
  • Modelling parametrically saves a lot of time later on with inter-part alignment

Augmented Reality Sandbox v2.0(SARndbox)

A sandbox that uses a projector, Xbox 360 Kinect, and powerful computer to project an augmented reality topographic map on the sand's surface.

The Augmented Reality Sandbox (SARndbox) is a project originally created by a team of motivated students at my high school, which was showcased at the LearnLaunch Conference at Harvard in 2016. Since then, it was sitting unused and the Director of Strategic Innovation wanted to bring it back to life with modification that would allow frequent usage during school hours.

Objectives:
  • Convert the SARndbox from movable to stationary
  • Make the frame more rigid by reinforcing it and reducing Kinect holder sag
  • Replacing the original projector with a different projector
  • Update the software and ensure it still works
Challenges:
  • Using Linux command line interface (CLI) to install programs
  • Calibrating the Kinect sensor to read the topographic surface of the sand
  • Accurately positioning the new projector
  • Minimizing cost by using only parts available in the makerspace
  • Creating an accurate CAD model of the sandbox
Results:
  • Director of Strategic Innovation is still showcasing the SARndbox today
  • SARndbox is still operational today
Takeaways:
  • Linux is extremely versatile and powerful, specifically with integrating many components together with software

NUROVER

A Mars rover made by 33 Northeastern students for competition in the University Rover Challenge (URC)

The Northeastern Mars Rover Team (NUROVER) was founded in September 2018, originally with the intention of competing in the following year's Mars Society University Rover Challenge (URC). The challenge requires the rover to partake in tasks emulating Mars' desert-like terrain, such as manipulating tools, interacting with objects, and autonomously navigating rocky terrain, all under a $18,000 budget and 50kg weight limit. More information can be found at the Mars Society's website here.

In the past three years that the team has competed, we've been accepted for field competition all years, achieving our highest Systems Acceptance Review (SAR) score in 2021. Our website can be found here.

2019-2020 Season - Watney MK2

I joined NUROVER in the 2020 season as part of the mobility team. This year, the mobility system was comprised of a 6-wheel drive rocker-bogie system. The rocker-bogie system is connected via a bar differential, which ensures that the rover can maintain at least 3 wheels on the ground even when traversing large obstacles. Compared to the previous year, the bar differential is a massive improvement since our implementation of a gear differential had significant backlash issues, rendering the rocker-bogie system significantly less effective. Each wheel is individually powered by a high-torque motor, allowing our rover to scale hilly terrain and slopes of over 45 degrees.

Mobility Objectives:
  • Implement a new carbon fiber bar differential system instead of gear differential
  • Outsource the manufacture of the rocker-bogie arches to reduce warping when bending the steel pipes
Challenges:
  • Understanding the forces that act on a bar differential, especially in 3D
  • Cutting carbon fiber rods
  • Getting quotes on bending steel pipes into arches
Results:
  • One of 36 teams accepted to compete in Utah (despite having in-person competition cancelled due to COVID19)
  • 84/100 score on the Systems Acceptance Review (SAR)
Lessons Learned:
  • Even a little bit of backlash or slop in a rocker-bogie system hinders it immensely
  • The bar differential worked better than the gear differential

2020-2021 Season - Watney MK2.1

As one of the mobility team leads for the 2021 competition year, my task was to oversee the design and manufacture of the frame of the rover, consisting of both the chassis and the mobility system. However, due to restrictions from COVID-19, the mobility team had limited to no access to university manufacturing or work spaces. Thus, our plans shifted to focus on testing the rover whenever possible and projects that do not require lab space, for example, fabricating 3D printed wheels.

Mobility Objectives:
  • Design 3D printable wheels that would be more rigid and cost effective than off-the-shelf (OTS) wheels
Challenges:
  • Determining an optimal tread pattern for the wheel
  • Getting access to university 3D printers (that were large enough) to print the wheels
  • Simulating forces on the wheel using SolidWorks FEA
Results:
  • Successfully printed and implemented 3D printed wheels on the rover
  • One of 36 teams accepted to compete in the remote competition
  • 89.1/100 score on the Systems Acceptance Review (SAR)
Lessons Learned:
  • Routine testing is very important, and a "fail fast" design philosophy might be good for next year
  • "Integration hell" is a real thing
  • 3D printed wheels might be more work than necessary, especially with evaluating tread pattern efficacy

2021 Wheel Showcase

Rendered Pan Around the 3D Printed Wheel

These 3D printed wheels are printed from 95A hardness TPU, which when combined with the honeycomb interior pattern, allow the wheel to be compliant, lightweight, and air-less. After much research into the tread pattern and creating multiple tread prototypes, we opted to go for a design similar to NASA's Curiosity rover. Additionally, using SolidWorks FEA, the maximum Von Mises stress that the wheel endures is less than 0.3 MPA, which is far below the 12 MPA tensile yield strength of the material.

Plastic Tuba

A cheap, 1-valved tuba created using PVC pipes and 3D printing to explore the feasibility of additive manufacturing in making musical instruments more accessible.

Click here to see an archive of some of my CAPStone work

After growing discontent with using the old, weathered tuba lent from my high school, I began to ponder about tubas and their accessibility. The cheap beginner tubas can cost over $1500, so it's not feasible to ask a new tuba player to purchase an instrument that they will outgrow should they choose to continue playing. It would be amazing to have a cheap, beginner tuba...

That's when I thought of combining 3D printing, a technology just entering the consumer space at the time that I was interested in, with other accessible parts to construct a tuba that would be cheap enough for a beginner player to use until they decide whether tuba is for them.

Image of me playing the tuba

Objectives:
  • Prototype a cost-effective beginner tuba using accessible means and parts
  • Explore new technologies like consumer 3D printing and how they can be applied to increase musical instrument accessibility
Challenges:
  • Maximizing 3D print quality from school printers
  • Reducing air leakage from 3D printed valves
  • Creating a tuba body using cheap, off-the-shelf parts
Results:
  • Constructed the tuba body using PVC pipes and connectors
  • Designed custom, 3D printable rotary valves that can function as brass instrument valves despite being leaky
  • The tuba can be played!
Takeaways:
  • Consumer 3D printers are currently unable to manufacture brass instrument valves to the tight tolerances they require, but I am hopeful that they may as the technology matures
  • Additive manufacturing offers unique methods of fabrication that will allow it to become more widespread in industrial and consumer settings

Practice Marimba

A 4.3 octave practice marimba that breathes life back into second-hand synthetic bars, combining woodworking and 3D printing.

I've always wanted to build my own marimba from raw materials due to being significantly cheaper than purchasing a new instrument. However, I have limited access to tools at my house, so while such an idea is not impossible, it is costly once tool purchasing costs are considered. One day, while browsing a used percussion equipment group, I found a listing for used marimba bars for $800. For context, the cheapest 4.3 octave marimba costs around $2500, with most of the cost coming from the skilled labor required for accurate tuning of marimba bars. I thought this was a great deal and perfect way to get a practice instrument, so I bought the bars and started working.

Image of the practice marimba

Objectives:
  • Construct a wooden frame to hold marimba bars
  • Design 3D printable posts to hold the paracord that the bars rest on
Challenges:
  • Determining the geometry of the through holes of the bars, and using it to angle each frame crossbeam
  • Drilling holes straight down without a drill press for the posts
Results:
  • Created a playable practice marimba
  • Saved several thousand dollars compared to purchasing a new marimba
Takeaways:
  • 3D printing can not only be used for fabricating parts, but also jigs to do work more easily and efficiently
  • Hand tools and power tools can do things that the other can not

SeaPerch

An underwater remotely-operated vehicle (ROV) designed with a limited budget to participate in the International SeaPerch Challenge.

SeaPerch is an underwater robotics competition that uses a budget-friendly remotely-operated vehicle (ROV) to traverse an obstacle course and complete pre-designated tasks, from lifting whiffle balls to opening hatches. The ROV is made using the base kit, which contains their default ROV, alongside $20 for off-the-shelf parts.

2018 Season - Hammerhead

Objectives:
  • Maximize the thrust out of the vertical lift motor
  • Maximize the ROV's speed
Design Choices:
  • Place the vertical lift motor in front of the rover to maximize the moment arm when manipulating objects
  • Use a T-shaped design (similar to a hammerhead shark) to increase hydrodynamics of the ROV
  • Use ducted propellors (Kort nozzles) on the back thrust motors to increase motor efficiency and provide more thrust
Challenges:
  • Designing effective ducted propellors
  • Attaching the right amount of floats and distributing them evenly enough to achieve neutral buoyancy
Results:
  • Placed 3rd internationally for engineering notebook and notebook presentation at the 2018 International SeaPerch Challenge
  • Designed cost-effective 3D printed Kort nozzles ($0.62, assuming 49.07 grams of filament at $25 / kg)
  • Achieved a faster ROV than most of the competitors
  • Featured in a local newspaper
Takeaways:
  • ROV frame weight primarily governs its speed, so it must be lighter to maximize speed

2019 Season - Jaws

Objectives:
  • Design a lighter, more hydrodynamic, and slimmer ROV frame than the previous year
  • Further develop CAD skills through more accurate modelling and learning fluid simulations
  • Trigger a beacon (Hall effect sensor) using magnets in some enclosure
Design Choices:
  • Use a flat design that minimizes front surface area
  • Preserve the Kort nozzles
  • Create many holes and slots in the beacon triggerer to make it more maneuverable through water
Challenges:
  • Learning how to use fluid simulations and interpret them
  • Attaching the right amount of floats and distributing them evenly enough to achieve neutral buoyancy
Results:
  • Selected to present our ROV in an additional engineering presentation due to our stellar engineering notebook
  • Placed 9th overall in the high school division of the 2019 International SeaPerch Challenge
  • Used Autodesk Flow to reduce the coefficient of drag from 0.92 to 0.72 by adding two motor caps, making the ROV more hydrodynamic (full documentation in the engineering notebook below)
  • Designed an effective beacon triggerer that was easy to manuever and manipulate
Takeaways:
  • Fluid simulations are an extremely important tool that can help engineers optimize designs
  • Engineering notebooks are our strong suit
