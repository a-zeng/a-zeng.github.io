Plastic Tuba

A cheap, 1-valved tuba created using PVC pipes and 3D printing to explore the feasibility of additive manufacturing in making musical instruments more accessible.

Click here to see an archive of some of my CAPStone work

After growing discontent with using the old, weathered tuba lent from my high school, I began to ponder about tubas and their accessibility. The cheap beginner tubas can cost over $1500, so it's not feasible to ask a new tuba player to purchase an instrument that they will outgrow should they choose to continue playing. It would be amazing to have a cheap, beginner tuba...

That's when I thought of combining 3D printing, a technology just entering the consumer space at the time that I was interested in, with other accessible parts to construct a tuba that would be cheap enough for a beginner player to use until they decide whether tuba is for them.

Image of me playing the tuba

Objectives:
  • Prototype a cost-effective beginner tuba using accessible means and parts
  • Explore new technologies like consumer 3D printing and how they can be applied to increase musical instrument accessibility
Challenges:
  • Maximizing 3D print quality from school printers
  • Reducing air leakage from 3D printed valves
  • Creating a tuba body using cheap, off-the-shelf parts
Results:
  • Constructed the tuba body using PVC pipes and connectors
  • Designed custom, 3D printable rotary valves that can function as brass instrument valves despite being leaky
  • The tuba can be played!
Takeaways:
  • Consumer 3D printers are currently unable to manufacture brass instrument valves to the tight tolerances they require, but I am hopeful that they may as the technology matures
  • Additive manufacturing offers unique methods of fabrication that will allow it to become more widespread in industrial and consumer settings
